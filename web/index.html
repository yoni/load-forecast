Heating and Cooling Load Forecast

Heating and cooling systems are by far the greatest consumers of energy in residential settings. Wouldn't it be nice if we could forecast how much energy people are likely to expend on heating and cooling?

Using the weather forecast, we can estimate heating and cooling load by using the concept of heating and cooling degree hours , known as HDH and CDH. The idea is to assume a comfortable temperature, say 65°F - we call this the balance point - and see how far the forecast temperature will be from that balance point.

The number of degrees that the actual temperature point falls below the balance point, we call heating degrees. Similarly, the number of degrees that the forecast temperature falls above the balance point, we call cooling degrees. In both cases, we disallow negative degree-hours, such that there will either be zero or more HDH and CDH.

Using weather forecast data from the National Digital Forecast Database (NDFD), we were able to implement the CDH/HDH balance-point algorithm for the entire United States, and plot the 7-day heating and cooling load forecast.

A good next step would be to introduce regional average set points for load estimation, using the EIA's average balance points.

Obviously, we could determine even more accurate load forecasts using state-of-the-art methodologies. If we were doing a deeper analysis, we might consider calculating heat buildup over time, humidity, and individual household set points.
